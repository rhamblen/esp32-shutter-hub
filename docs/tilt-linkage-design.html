    Linkage — design variables

    1Why this linkage — the correction

    Earlier project docs sized the linkage as if the shutter had a venetian-blind style twisting wand (a few mm of pushrod travel). A plantation hidden tilt rod is different: the rod hangs off the rear corner of every slat and translates without rotating. Every point of the rod rides a circular arc of radius R ≈ 36 mm centred on its slat's pivot. Going from fully closed to open the rod's bottom pin moves ~36 mm up and ~36 mm outward — a point-to-point stroke of ~51 mm. The old 10 mm-horn : 20 mm-arm plan delivered ~16 mm and could never have worked.

    The mechanism is therefore a genuine four-bar: servo arm (crank) → ball-link pushrod (coupler) → slat corner (rocker, radius R). The simulator above solves the exact kinematics, tracks the assembly branch (several plausible-looking layouts turn out to run off along the wrong branch and jam — they are rejected automatically), and checks collisions against the bottom rail, the moving slats and the rear clearance to the glass.

    2Two workable layouts (28 mm frame, measured)

    With the frame measured at 28 mm thick, the mortised-pocket concept is dead (a 39 mm pocket cannot live in a 28 mm rail) — but surface mounting works on either face of the bottom rail. Both layouts drive a printed slat-hook bracket (clips over the bottom slat — no screws in the slat, no connection to the flush-fitting tilt rod) and are near-perfect mirror images. The one physical difference: each mount only works with one slat closure direction.

    3Measure these before printing

    1. R — slat pivot pin to tilt-rod staple centre (assumed 36 mm).
    2. Slat pitch — pivot to pivot (assumed 63 mm).
    3. Panel / rail thickness — front face to rear face (measured: 28 mm).
    4. Rail top below pivot — bottom slat pivot down to the top edge of the bottom rail (assumed 45 mm). This decides whether the rail pocket is needed at all.
    5. Rear clearance — slat-pivot plane to glass / window frame (assumed ≥46 mm; the rod itself already needs ~40).
    6. Rod plane offset — stile inner face to tilt-rod centreline (sets the bracket's sideways stand-off; not in the 2-D sim).
    7. Rod cross-section — width × thickness of the tilt rod (sizes the clamp adapter).
    8. Rod pull force — hook a luggage/kitchen scale on the tilt rod and pull along its travel. Target ≤ 0.6 kg; if higher, slacken the slat tension screws.

    Feed 1–5 into the simulator above and re-check the flags before committing to prints.

    4Mounting, rotation direction & the window frame

    The servo output shaft must be parallel to the slat pivot axes (rotation in the same plane as the slats). On a rail-face mount the body lies flat along the rail, so both shaft directions fit — point the shaft inboard (toward the panel centre) so the horn plane lands on the slat-hook plane, with the body extending toward the stile. Flipping the servo changes nothing kinematically — only the rotation sense reverses, and the firmware's per-shutter closed/open µs endpoints absorb that entirely (closed simply gets the other end of the pulse range). No code change, no new maths.

    Sideways (plane) offset: the slat pivots live inside the stile, so a stile-side servo would sit ~20 mm off the crank's plane. That is tolerable within limits: a constant out-of-plane offset dz just shortens the pushrod's effective in-plane length to √(L² − dz²) (enter that value as the pushrod length in the simulator) and the ball links run at a fixed angle atan(dz/L). Nylon M2 ball links articulate ~±15°, so keep dz ≤ L/4 and split the angle by tilting both ball studs. The recommended mounts avoid the issue entirely: the cradle sits on the rail face, and the horn's position along the shaft direction is set by the bracket — slide it to put the horn plane on the crank plane (dz ≈ 0).

    Why a slat hook, not a rod clamp: the tilt rod sits flush against the slat edges / frame at closed, leaving nothing to grip, and its pin is locked at 36 mm radius, phase 0. A bracket hooked over the bottom slat (like the Zemismart-style clip) is a proper crank whose radius and phase are free — that freedom is what makes both surface-mount layouts solvable at all. It also comes off without a trace.

    Slat hook vs the tilt rod (rear mount): in x–y the pushrod and the hanging tilt rod cross paths, but they live in different vertical planes — put the hook (and therefore the horn plane) ≥10 mm inboard of the rod's plane and the rod passes beside the linkage, never through it.

    Two more fit constraints: mount everything on the panel (never bridge panel → frame, or the panel can no longer hinge open for cleaning) and leave a flexible service loop in the servo lead at the hinge side. The horn plane must line up with the crank plane to within ~2 mm — the ball links absorb small residual misalignment (~5°).

    7Torque budget

    MG90D usable torque ≈ 2.2 kg·cm (stall 2.4–2.8). Worst-case transmission ratio ≤0.84 (A) / ≤0.86 (B) means ≥ ~2.6 kg·cm available at the slat crank — equivalent to ~0.7 kg of tangential force at the tilt rod's 36 mm radius. The measured rod pull force (checklist item 8) must come in under ~0.6 kg for comfortable margin; the slat tension screws are the adjustment if it doesn't. Peak coupler loads stay well inside the M2 ball links' rating in both options.
